## 1. C-Linear Closure Proposition

**Setup.** The Clifford algebra $\mathrm{Cl}(9,0)$ acts on $V_{1/2} = \mathbb{R}^{16}$ via the generators $T_1, \ldots, T_9$ satisfying $\{T_a, T_b\} = \tfrac{1}{2}\delta_{ab}I_{16}$. The standard monomial basis of $\mathrm{Cl}(9,0)$ restricted to the $\omega = +1$ sector (see Section 2) consists of all ordered products

$$
T_S \;=\; T_{a_1} T_{a_2} \cdots T_{a_k}, \quad S = \{a_1, \ldots, a_k\} \subseteq \{1, \ldots, 9\}, \quad a_1 < a_2 < \cdots < a_k,
$$

with $T_\emptyset = I_{16}$. There are $2^9 = 512$ such monomials. However, since the representation on $V_{1/2}$ factors through the $\omega = +1$ summand of $\mathrm{Cl}(9,0) \cong M_{16}(\mathbb{R}) \oplus M_{16}(\mathbb{R})$, these 512 monomials span $M_{16}(\mathbb{R})$ as an $\mathbb{R}$-vector space. Specifically, the even-grade monomials ($|S|$ even) already form a basis of $M_{16}(\mathbb{R})$: there are $\sum_{k \text{ even}} \binom{9}{k} = 2^8 = 256$ of them, and they are $\mathbb{R}$-linearly independent (Phase 29; $\mathrm{Cl}^+(9,0)$ in the $\omega = +1$ sector equals $M_{16}(\mathbb{R})$).

**Lemma (R-independence implies C-independence).** *Let $\{v_1, \ldots, v_N\}$ be $\mathbb{R}$-linearly independent vectors in $\mathbb{R}^d$. Viewing them as elements of $\mathbb{C}^d$ via the standard inclusion $\mathbb{R}^d \subset \mathbb{C}^d$, they are $\mathbb{C}$-linearly independent.*

**Proof.** Suppose $\sum_{j=1}^N (\alpha_j + i\beta_j)\,v_j = 0$ with $\alpha_j, \beta_j \in \mathbb{R}$. Separating real and imaginary parts:

$$
\sum_{j=1}^N \alpha_j\,v_j = 0, \qquad \sum_{j=1}^N \beta_j\,v_j = 0.
$$

Since $v_1, \ldots, v_N$ are $\mathbb{R}$-linearly independent, both equations force $\alpha_j = 0$ and $\beta_j = 0$ for all $j$. $\square$

**Proposition (C-Linear Closure).** *The $\mathbb{C}$-linear span of the 256 even-grade $\mathrm{Cl}(9,0)$ monomials $\{T_S : S \subseteq \{1, \ldots, 9\},\; |S| \text{ even}\}$ equals $M_{16}(\mathbb{C})$.*

**Proof.**

1. The 256 even-grade monomials are $\mathbb{R}$-linearly independent in $M_{16}(\mathbb{R})$ (they form a basis of $\mathrm{Cl}^+(9,0)$ restricted to $V_{1/2}$, which equals $M_{16}(\mathbb{R})$; Phase 29).

2. View each monomial $T_S \in M_{16}(\mathbb{R}) \subset M_{16}(\mathbb{C})$ as a complex matrix (with zero imaginary part). By the Lemma, these 256 matrices are $\mathbb{C}$-linearly independent in $M_{16}(\mathbb{C})$.

3. $\dim_\mathbb{C} M_{16}(\mathbb{C}) = 16^2 = 256$.

4. Since we have 256 $\mathbb{C}$-linearly independent elements in a 256-dimensional $\mathbb{C}$-vector space, they form a $\mathbb{C}$-basis and span all of $M_{16}(\mathbb{C})$. $\square$

## 2. Cl(9,C) Identification

**Standard classification (Lawson-Michelsohn, *Spin Geometry*, 1989, Table I.4.3):**

$$
\mathrm{Cl}(9,\mathbb{C}) \;=\; \mathrm{Cl}(9,0) \otimes_\mathbb{R} \mathbb{C} \;\cong\; M_{16}(\mathbb{C}) \oplus M_{16}(\mathbb{C}).
$$

This is the general pattern for odd $n$: $\mathrm{Cl}(n,\mathbb{C}) \cong M_{2^{(n-1)/2}}(\mathbb{C}) \oplus M_{2^{(n-1)/2}}(\mathbb{C})$ for $n$ odd. For $n = 9$: $2^{(9-1)/2} = 2^4 = 16$.

**Volume element.** The volume element of $\mathrm{Cl}(9,0)$ is

$$
\omega \;=\; T_1\,T_2\,\cdots\,T_9.
$$

Since $n = 9$ is odd and the signature is $(9,0)$, one has $\omega^2 = (-1)^{9(9-1)/2} \cdot (1/2)^9 \cdot I$... but this uses the non-standard normalization. Let us compute directly using $T_a = \tfrac{1}{2}\gamma_a$:

$$
\omega = T_1 T_2 \cdots T_9 = \frac{1}{2^9}\,\gamma_1 \gamma_2 \cdots \gamma_9.
$$

For $\mathrm{Cl}(9,0)$ with $\{\gamma_a, \gamma_b\} = 2\delta_{ab}$, the volume element $\gamma_1 \cdots \gamma_9$ satisfies $(\gamma_1 \cdots \gamma_9)^2 = (-1)^{9 \cdot 8/2} I = (-1)^{36} I = +I$, and on the irreducible $16$-dimensional summand, $\gamma_1 \cdots \gamma_9 = \pm I_{16}$.

In the $T_a$ normalization: $\omega = (1/2^9)(\gamma_1 \cdots \gamma_9)$. Since $\gamma_1 \cdots \gamma_9 = c \cdot I_{16}$ with $c = \pm 1$ on the irreducible summand, we get $\omega = c/2^9 \cdot I_{16}$.

However, in the representation on $V_{1/2}$, the relevant quantity is the *representation-level* volume element. The $\mathrm{Cl}(9,0)$ monomials acting on $V_{1/2}$ factor through the $\omega = +1$ sector, where $\omega$ here means the ALGEBRA-level volume element evaluates to $+1$. The direct computational check (Task 2 below, and Phase 42 infrastructure) will verify the sign.

**Correction:** The volume element as typically defined in Clifford algebras for the purpose of splitting $\mathrm{Cl}(n,0)$ into two summands uses the *gamma* generators, not the $T_a$. Define $\hat\omega = \gamma_1 \gamma_2 \cdots \gamma_9 = 2^9 \cdot T_1 T_2 \cdots T_9$. Then $\hat\omega^2 = I$ on any irreducible module, and the two summands of $\mathrm{Cl}(9,0) \cong M_{16}(\mathbb{R}) \oplus M_{16}(\mathbb{R})$ correspond to the $\hat\omega = +1$ and $\hat\omega = -1$ eigenspaces. The representation on $V_{1/2}$ is the $\hat\omega = +1$ summand, meaning $\hat\omega$ acts as $+I_{16}$ on $V_{1/2}$.

In terms of the $T_a$: $T_1 T_2 \cdots T_9 = (1/2^9)\hat\omega = (1/512)\hat\omega$, so on $V_{1/2}$:

$$
T_1\,T_2\,\cdots\,T_9 = \frac{1}{512}\,I_{16}.
$$

This will be verified computationally in Task 2.

## 3. Spinor Module Extension $S_9 \to S_{10}^+$

**Step 1.** $V_{1/2} = \mathbb{R}^{16}$ is the unique irreducible real $\mathrm{Spin}(9)$ spinor module $S_9$ (Phase 29; Lawson-Michelsohn, *Spin Geometry*, Ch. I.5). Dimension: $\dim_\mathbb{R}(S_9) = 2^{(9-1)/2} = 16$.

**Step 2.** Complexification: $S_9 \otimes_\mathbb{R} \mathbb{C} = \mathbb{C}^{16}$.

**Step 3.** $\mathrm{Spin}(10)$ Weyl representations (Lawson-Michelsohn, Ch. I.5): $\mathrm{Spin}(10)$ has two Weyl representations $S_{10}^+$ and $S_{10}^-$, each of $\mathbb{C}$-dimension $2^{10/2-1} = 2^4 = 16$. These are the irreducible chiral spinor representations.

**Step 4.** Branching rule (Lawson-Michelsohn, Ch. I.5, standard result for $\mathrm{Spin}(n) \subset \mathrm{Spin}(n+1)$): Under the inclusion $\mathrm{Spin}(9) \subset \mathrm{Spin}(10)$,

$$
S_{10}^+\big|_{\mathrm{Spin}(9)} \;\cong\; S_9 \otimes_\mathbb{R} \mathbb{C}.
$$

That is, the positive-chirality Weyl spinor of $\mathrm{Spin}(10)$, restricted to $\mathrm{Spin}(9)$, is the complexification of the real spinor $S_9$.

**Step 5.** Therefore:

$$
V_{1/2}^\mathbb{C} \;=\; S_9 \otimes_\mathbb{R} \mathbb{C} \;=\; \mathbb{C}^{16} \;\cong\; S_{10}^+.
$$

The complexified Peirce half-eigenspace is identified with the positive-chirality Weyl spinor of $\mathrm{Spin}(10)$.

**Step 6.** The chirality ($+$ vs $-$) is fixed by the volume element convention. We chose $\hat\omega = +I_{16}$ on $V_{1/2}$ (Section 2). The $\mathrm{Spin}(10)$ chirality operator is $\gamma_{11} = \gamma_1 \cdots \gamma_{10}$. Under $\mathrm{Spin}(9) \subset \mathrm{Spin}(10)$, the restriction to $S_9^\mathbb{C}$ gives: the $\gamma_{11} = +1$ eigenspace corresponds to the $\hat\omega = +1$ sector of $\mathrm{Cl}(9,0)$. Since $V_{1/2}$ is in the $\hat\omega = +1$ sector, we identify $V_{1/2}^\mathbb{C} = S_{10}^+$ (positive chirality).

**Physical interpretation.** The observer's complex functional calculus (Plan 01) complexifies the $\mathrm{Spin}(9)$ spinor module $S_9 = \mathbb{R}^{16}$ to the $\mathrm{Spin}(10)$ positive-chirality Weyl spinor $S_{10}^+ = \mathbb{C}^{16}$. This $16$-dimensional complex representation is the **16** of $\mathrm{Spin}(10)$, which under the $\mathrm{SO}(10) \supset \mathrm{SU}(5)$ branching contains one generation of Standard Model fermion content. The complexification requires the observer's C*-structure -- it cannot arise from $\mathrm{Cl}(9,0)$ alone ($\mathrm{End}_{\mathrm{Spin}(9)}(S_9) = \mathbb{R}$, Phase 30 Theorem 1).
