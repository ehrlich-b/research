## 1. Setup and Definitions

Let $\mathfrak{h}_3(\mathbb{O})$ denote the exceptional Jordan algebra of $3 \times 3$ Hermitian octonionic matrices. Fix a diagonal idempotent $e = E_{11}$. The Peirce decomposition with respect to $e$ is

$$
\mathfrak{h}_3(\mathbb{O}) = V_1 \oplus V_{1/2} \oplus V_0,
$$

where $V_\lambda = \{x \in \mathfrak{h}_3(\mathbb{O}) : e \circ x = \lambda x\}$ and $\circ$ denotes the Jordan product $a \circ b = \tfrac{1}{2}(ab + ba)$.

The half-eigenspace is $V_{1/2} \cong \mathbb{R}^{16}$. The Peirce multiplication operators $T_a : V_{1/2} \to V_{1/2}$ for $a = 1, \ldots, 9$ are defined by the action of nine octonionic directions and satisfy the Clifford relation

$$
\{T_a, T_b\} = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\delta_{ab}\,I_{16}, \quad a, b = 1, \ldots, 9,
$$

where $T_a = \tfrac{1}{2}\gamma_a$ with $\gamma_a$ the standard $\mathrm{Cl}(9,0)$ generators satisfying $\{\gamma_a, \gamma_b\} = 2\delta_{ab}I_{16}$. In particular, $T_a^2 = \tfrac{1}{4}I_{16}$, so each $T_a$ has spectrum $\{+\tfrac{1}{2}, -\tfrac{1}{2}\}$.

The Clifford algebra generated by $T_1, \ldots, T_9$ is

$$
\mathrm{Cl}(9,0) \;\cong\; M_{16}(\mathbb{R}) \oplus M_{16}(\mathbb{R}),
$$

and the action on $V_{1/2} = \mathbb{R}^{16}$ restricts to one irreducible summand, so the operators $\{T_a\}$ generate $M_{16}(\mathbb{R})$ as an $\mathbb{R}$-algebra (Phase 29).

**The observer.** By the self-modeling theorem (Paper 5, Theorem 3.1 [type-exclusion.tex, lines 205--246]), a finite-dimensional system admitting a faithful self-model with non-signaling composite and product-form sequential product inheritance has state space

$$
V_{\mathrm{obs}} \cong M_n(\mathbb{C})^{\mathrm{sa}} \quad \text{for some } n \ge 2,
$$

with sequential product given by the Luders rule

$$
a\, \&\, b \;=\; \sqrt{a}\; b\; \sqrt{a}.
$$

This is the unique C*-algebra structure compatible with the self-modeling axioms; the square root is computed via the continuous functional calculus of the ambient C*-algebra $M_n(\mathbb{C})$.


## 2. Lemma (Faithful Embedding)

**Lemma.** *The observer faithfully represents the Peirce operators $T_a$ as self-adjoint elements of its algebra. More precisely: there exists an injective unital $*$-homomorphism*

$$
\iota : M_{16}(\mathbb{R}) \hookrightarrow M_n(\mathbb{C})
$$

*for $n \ge 16$, such that $\iota(T_a)$ is self-adjoint in $M_n(\mathbb{C})$ for each $a = 1, \ldots, 9$.*

**Proof.**

(i) The observer measures the physical degrees of freedom carried by $V_{1/2}$. Since the $T_a$ encode the nine independent measurement directions in $V_{1/2}$ (the Peirce multiplication operators), the observer's measurement algebra must contain operators representing each $T_a$.

(ii) Faithful representation requires that $\iota$ be injective on the algebra generated by the $T_a$, which is $M_{16}(\mathbb{R})$. As a 256-dimensional real algebra, faithful representation on $\mathbb{C}^n$ requires $n^2 \ge 256$, hence $n \ge 16$.

(iii) The standard inclusion $\iota : M_{16}(\mathbb{R}) \hookrightarrow M_{16}(\mathbb{C})$ views each real matrix as a complex matrix with zero imaginary part. This is an injective unital $*$-homomorphism: for $A \in M_{16}(\mathbb{R})$,

$$
\iota(A)^\dagger = \overline{\iota(A)}^T = \iota(A)^T = \iota(A^T).
$$

Since each $T_a$ is real and symmetric ($T_a^T = T_a$, being a Clifford generator in the real representation), we have

$$
\iota(T_a)^\dagger = \iota(T_a^T) = \iota(T_a).
$$

Therefore $\iota(T_a)$ is self-adjoint (Hermitian) in $M_{16}(\mathbb{C}) \subseteq M_n(\mathbb{C})$.

(iv) For $n > 16$, the embedding is $\iota : M_{16}(\mathbb{R}) \hookrightarrow M_n(\mathbb{C})$ via $A \mapsto \mathrm{diag}(A, 0_{n-16})$ (block-diagonal with zero padding). The restriction to the $V_{1/2}$ sector is the $16 \times 16$ upper-left block, which is faithful. $\square$

**Remark.** The argument does not require $n = 16$. The minimal faithful dimension is $n = 16$; the theorem holds for all $n \ge 16$. In what follows, we work with the restriction to the $V_{1/2}$ sector, which lives in $M_{16}(\mathbb{C})$ regardless of $n$.


## 3. Proposition (Extended Sequential Product)

**Proposition.** *In a C*-algebra $A$, the Luders sequential product $a\, \&\, b = \sqrt{a}\,b\,\sqrt{a}$ extends naturally from the Gudder--Greechie effect domain to all self-adjoint elements with non-zero spectrum. Specifically:*

*(i) For effects $e \in [0, I]$ (spectrum $\subseteq [0,1]$): $\sqrt{e}$ is the unique positive square root, computed via the positive functional calculus. The result $\sqrt{e}\,b\,\sqrt{e}$ is real-valued when $b$ is self-adjoint.*

*(ii) For general self-adjoint $a$ with $\mathrm{spec}(a) \subseteq \mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}$: $\sqrt{a}$ is defined via the continuous functional calculus of $A$, applying the principal branch of $\sqrt{\cdot}$ (with $\mathrm{Re}(\sqrt{z}) \ge 0$). For eigenvalue $\lambda < 0$, this gives $\sqrt{\lambda} = i\sqrt{|\lambda|}$. The product $\sqrt{a}\,b\,\sqrt{a}$ is well-defined but may be complex-valued (not self-adjoint).*

*(iii) The extended product is compatible with the Gudder--Greechie product on effects: for $a \in [0, I]$, the two definitions coincide.*

**Proof.**

(i) For $e \in [0,I]$, the spectrum lies in $[0,1]$, so $\sqrt{\cdot}$ on $[0,1]$ is the standard non-negative real-valued square root. This is the Gudder--Greechie framework (Rep. Math. Phys. 49:87--111, 2002): the sequential product $e\,\&\,b = \sqrt{e}\,b\,\sqrt{e}$ is defined for effects and yields a self-adjoint result when $b$ is self-adjoint.

(ii) For self-adjoint $a$ with eigenvalue $\lambda < 0$, the real-valued square root $\sqrt{\lambda}$ is undefined. In a *complex* C*-algebra $A$, the continuous functional calculus for self-adjoint elements is the unique $*$-homomorphism $\Phi_a : C(\mathrm{spec}(a)) \to A$ with $\Phi_a(\mathrm{id}) = a$. Since $\mathrm{spec}(a) \subset \mathbb{R}$, we can apply any continuous function $f : \mathrm{spec}(a) \to \mathbb{C}$. Taking $f = \sqrt{\cdot}$ with the principal branch:

$$
\sqrt{\lambda} = \begin{cases} \sqrt{\lambda} & \text{if } \lambda > 0 \\ i\sqrt{|\lambda|} & \text{if } \lambda < 0 \end{cases}
$$

This defines $\sqrt{a} \in A$ (not necessarily self-adjoint). The product $\sqrt{a}\,b\,\sqrt{a}$ is then a well-defined element of $A$.

(iii) When $\mathrm{spec}(a) \subseteq [0,1]$, the principal branch $\sqrt{\cdot}$ agrees with the non-negative square root on $[0,1]$. $\square$

**Critical observation.** The Peirce operator $T_a$ has eigenvalue $-\tfrac{1}{2} < 0$. Therefore $T_a \notin [0, I]$: it is NOT an effect. The Gudder--Greechie framework (Rep. Math. Phys. 49:87--111, 2002) does not cover $T_a\,\&\,T_b$. The observer's C*-structure provides the necessary extension.

**Phase 42 evidence.** The domain distinction is not merely formal; it has concrete consequences:

- **Effect control** (72 pairs verified): $E_a = \tfrac{1}{2}(I_{16} + 2T_a)$ is an effect with spectrum $\{0, 1\}$. The sequential product gives $E_a\,\&\,E_b = \sqrt{E_a}\,E_b\,\sqrt{E_a} = E_a\,E_b\,E_a = \tfrac{1}{2}E_a$, entirely real. Imaginary Frobenius norm: exactly $0.00$ (Phase 42, all 72 off-diagonal pairs).

- **Non-effect result** (72 pairs verified): $T_a$ is NOT an effect. The sequential product gives $T_a\,\&\,T_b = \sqrt{T_a}\,T_b\,\sqrt{T_a} = \tfrac{i}{2}T_b$, genuinely complex. Imaginary Frobenius norm: $\ge 1.000$ (Phase 42, all 72 anticommuting pairs; NumPy max error $2.23 \times 10^{-16}$, SymPy exact zero residual).


## 4. Theorem (Observer-Induced Complexification) -- Route A

**Theorem (Observer-Induced Complexification).** *Let $V_{1/2} = \mathbb{R}^{16}$ carry the $\mathrm{Cl}(9,0)$ generators $T_a$ $(a = 1, \ldots, 9)$ with $\{T_a, T_b\} = \tfrac{1}{2}\delta_{ab}I_{16}$, and let the observer's algebra be $M_n(\mathbb{C})^{\mathrm{sa}}$ $(n \ge 16)$ with the Luders sequential product (Paper 5, Theorem 3.1). Then:*

*(i) The observer's sequential product on the embedded $T_a$ uses the holomorphic functional calculus of $M_n(\mathbb{C})$, yielding*

$$
\sqrt{T_a} \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\bigl(P_+ + i\,P_-\bigr),
$$

*where $P_\pm$ are the spectral projections of $T_a$ onto the eigenspaces of $\pm\tfrac{1}{2}$.*

*(ii) For anticommuting pairs ($\{T_a, T_b\} = 0$, $a \ne b$):*

$$
\sqrt{T_a}\; T_b\; \sqrt{T_a} \;=\; \frac{i}{2}\,T_b.
$$

*(iii) The sequential product exits $M_{16}(\mathbb{R})$: $\mathrm{Im}\bigl(\sqrt{T_a}\,T_b\,\sqrt{T_a}\bigr) \ne 0$ for all 72 anticommuting pairs.*

**Proof (Route A -- Holomorphic Functional Calculus).**

**Step 1.** The observer is $M_n(\mathbb{C})^{\mathrm{sa}}$ (Paper 5, Theorem 3.1). By the Faithful Embedding Lemma (Section 2), $T_a$ is a self-adjoint element of $M_n(\mathbb{C})$ with spectrum $\{+\tfrac{1}{2}, -\tfrac{1}{2}\}$. The spectral decomposition is

$$
T_a = \tfrac{1}{2}\,P_+ - \tfrac{1}{2}\,P_-,
$$

where $P_+ + P_- = I_{16}$, $P_+ P_- = 0$, and $P_\pm$ are rank-8 orthogonal projections.

**Step 2.** In the C*-algebra $M_n(\mathbb{C})$, the continuous functional calculus for self-adjoint $T_a$ applies $f : \mathrm{spec}(T_a) \to \mathbb{C}$ via

$$
f(T_a) = f(\tfrac{1}{2})\,P_+ + f(-\tfrac{1}{2})\,P_-.
$$

Take $f = \sqrt{\cdot}$ with the principal branch ($\mathrm{Re}(\sqrt{z}) \ge 0$):

$$
\sqrt{\tfrac{1}{2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \qquad \sqrt{-\tfrac{1}{2}} = \frac{i}{\sqrt{2}}.
$$

The second value follows from the principal branch: $\sqrt{z} = |z|^{1/2}\,e^{i\arg(z)/2}$ with $\arg(-\tfrac{1}{2}) = \pi$, giving $e^{i\pi/2} = i$. Therefore

$$
\sqrt{T_a} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\,P_+ + \frac{i}{\sqrt{2}}\,P_- = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(P_+ + i\,P_-).
$$

SELF-CRITIQUE CHECKPOINT (Step 2):
1. SIGN CHECK: sqrt(1/2) = +1/sqrt(2), sqrt(-1/2) = +i/sqrt(2). Both positive real part. Expected: yes.
2. FACTOR CHECK: Factor of 1/sqrt(2) from sqrt(1/2). No spurious 2, pi, hbar, c.
3. CONVENTION CHECK: Principal branch with Re(sqrt(z)) >= 0. Matches convention lock.
4. DIMENSION CHECK: T_a is dimensionless (pure algebra), sqrt(T_a) is dimensionless. Consistent.

**Verification of Step 2:** $(\sqrt{T_a})^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}P_+ + \tfrac{i^2}{2}P_- = \tfrac{1}{2}P_+ - \tfrac{1}{2}P_- = T_a$. $\checkmark$

**Step 3: Why there is no real alternative.** The real-valued square root function $\sqrt{\cdot} : \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0} \to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$ is defined only for non-negative arguments. Since $T_a$ has eigenvalue $-\tfrac{1}{2} < 0$, NO real-valued functional calculus can produce $\sqrt{T_a}$.

If the observer were $M_n(\mathbb{R})^{\mathrm{sa}}$ (a real C*-algebra), the functional calculus would be restricted to real-valued functions on the spectrum, and $\sqrt{T_a}$ would be undefined. But Paper 5 proves the observer is $M_n(\mathbb{C})^{\mathrm{sa}}$, not $M_n(\mathbb{R})^{\mathrm{sa}}$. The complex C*-algebra $M_n(\mathbb{C})$ has the continuous functional calculus accepting $\mathbb{C}$-valued functions on the real spectrum. The principal-branch square root is the unique square root in $M_n(\mathbb{C})$ satisfying the convention $\mathrm{Re}(\sqrt{\lambda}) \ge 0$ for all eigenvalues $\lambda$.

There is no alternative: the observer MUST use the complex functional calculus. The complex square root is not a choice; it is the only one available.

**Step 4: Computing the sequential product.** For anticommuting $T_a, T_b$ ($a \ne b$, $\{T_a, T_b\} = 0$):

$$
\sqrt{T_a}\; T_b\; \sqrt{T_a} = \frac{1}{2}(P_+ + iP_-)\,T_b\,(P_+ + iP_-).
$$

Expand:

$$
= \frac{1}{2}\bigl(P_+ T_b P_+ + i\,P_+ T_b P_- + i\,P_- T_b P_+ + i^2\,P_- T_b P_-\bigr).
$$

Now use the anticommutation $\{T_a, T_b\} = 0$. Since $T_a = \tfrac{1}{2}(P_+ - P_-)$, we have

$$
T_a T_b + T_b T_a = 0 \implies (P_+ - P_-)T_b + T_b(P_+ - P_-) = 0.
$$

Using $P_+ + P_- = I$, decompose $T_b = P_+ T_b P_+ + P_+ T_b P_- + P_- T_b P_+ + P_- T_b P_-$. The anticommutation relation gives:

$$
P_+ T_b P_+ - P_- T_b P_+ + P_+ T_b P_- - P_- T_b P_- + P_+ T_b P_+ + P_- T_b P_+ - P_+ T_b P_- - P_- T_b P_- = 0.
$$

Collecting terms:

$$
2\,P_+ T_b P_+ - 2\,P_- T_b P_- = 0 \implies P_+ T_b P_+ = P_- T_b P_-.
$$

Also, $\mathrm{tr}(T_b) = 0$ (since $T_b$ is a Clifford generator with equal-rank $\pm\tfrac{1}{2}$ eigenspaces), and $\mathrm{tr}(P_\pm T_b P_\pm) = \mathrm{tr}(T_b P_\pm)$. Computing via the Clifford relation: $T_a T_b = -T_b T_a$ implies $P_+ T_b P_+ = P_- T_b P_- = 0$ (each diagonal block of $T_b$ in the eigenbasis of $T_a$ vanishes, because anticommuting Hermitian matrices have purely off-diagonal structure in each other's eigenbasis). To see this directly: $T_b P_+ = T_b \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}(I + 2T_a) = \tfrac{1}{2}T_b + T_b T_a = \tfrac{1}{2}T_b - T_a T_b$. Then

$$
P_+ T_b P_+ = P_+ (\tfrac{1}{2}T_b - T_a T_b) = \tfrac{1}{2}P_+ T_b - P_+ T_a T_b = \tfrac{1}{2}P_+ T_b - \tfrac{1}{2}P_+ T_b = 0,
$$

using $P_+ T_a = \tfrac{1}{2}P_+$ (since $P_+$ projects onto the $+\tfrac{1}{2}$ eigenspace of $T_a$). Similarly $P_- T_b P_- = 0$.

SELF-CRITIQUE CHECKPOINT (Step 4):
1. SIGN CHECK: P_+ T_a = (1/2)P_+. Then P_+ T_a T_b = (1/2)P_+ T_b. And P_+ T_b P_+ = (1/2)P_+ T_b - P_+ T_a T_b = (1/2)P_+ T_b - (1/2)P_+ T_b = 0. Signs consistent.
2. FACTOR CHECK: No new factors of 2, pi, etc. introduced. The 1/2 comes from the spectral decomposition.
3. CONVENTION CHECK: Using {T_a, T_b} = (1/2)delta_{ab} with delta_{ab} = 0 for a != b. Matches convention lock.
4. DIMENSION CHECK: All operators are dimensionless 16x16 matrices. Consistent.

Therefore the off-diagonal blocks are the only nonzero parts: $T_b = P_+ T_b P_- + P_- T_b P_+$.

Substituting back into the expansion:

$$
\sqrt{T_a}\; T_b\; \sqrt{T_a} = \frac{1}{2}\bigl(0 + i\,P_+ T_b P_- + i\,P_- T_b P_+ + 0\bigr) = \frac{i}{2}\bigl(P_+ T_b P_- + P_- T_b P_+\bigr) = \frac{i}{2}\,T_b.
$$

This completes the proof of (ii). $\square$

**Step 5: Verification via Phase 42.** Statement (ii) has been verified computationally for all 72 anticommuting pairs of $\mathrm{Cl}(9,0)$ generators:

- **NumPy** (float64): $\max_{a \ne b} \|\sqrt{T_a}\,T_b\,\sqrt{T_a} - \tfrac{i}{2}T_b\|_F = 2.23 \times 10^{-16}$ (machine epsilon).
- **SymPy** (exact rational arithmetic): residual is exactly the zero matrix for all 72 pairs.
- **Imaginary exit**: $\min_{a \ne b} \|\mathrm{Im}(\sqrt{T_a}\,T_b\,\sqrt{T_a})\|_F = 1.000$, confirming nonzero imaginary part.

(Phase 42, Plan 01, `code/verify_sequential_product.py`; all 153 pair verifications passed.)


## 5. Route B Sketch (Alfsen--Shultz Dynamical Correspondence)

The following provides an independent route to the same conclusion. It is a *sketch*, not a complete proof; the full construction is deferred unless Route A is deemed insufficient.

**Background.** The Alfsen--Shultz theorem (PNAS 95:6596, 1998; CMP 194:87--108, 1998) states that a JB-algebra $A$ is the self-adjoint part of a C*-algebra if and only if $A$ admits a *dynamical correspondence*: a map $\psi : A \to \mathrm{Der}(A)$ satisfying certain axioms. When such a $\psi$ exists, the C*-product is recovered as

$$
ab = a \circ b - i\,\psi_b(a),
$$

where $\circ$ is the Jordan product and $\psi_b$ is a derivation (Lie algebraic part).

**Application.** $M_{16}(\mathbb{R})^{\mathrm{sa}}$ is a JB-algebra. It is the self-adjoint part of both $M_{16}(\mathbb{R})$ (a real C*-algebra) and $M_{16}(\mathbb{C})$ (a complex C*-algebra). The two structures correspond to different dynamical correspondences:

- **Real structure** ($M_{16}(\mathbb{R})$): $\psi$ trivial (no imaginary Lie part), the associative product is the matrix product itself.
- **Complex structure** ($M_{16}(\mathbb{C})$): $\psi_b(a) = \tfrac{1}{2i}[a, b]$ provides the nontrivial dynamical correspondence, giving $ab = a \circ b + \tfrac{1}{2}[a,b]$ in the real algebra, which extends to $ab = a \circ b - i \cdot \tfrac{1}{2i}[a,b] = a \circ b + \tfrac{1}{2}[a,b]$ -- wait, the complex C*-product on $M_{16}(\mathbb{C})$ gives $ab = a \circ b + \tfrac{1}{2}[a,b]$ (same formula). The distinction is that the complex C*-algebra $M_{16}(\mathbb{C})$ is a LARGER algebra containing $M_{16}(\mathbb{R})^{\mathrm{sa}}$, and the imaginary unit $i$ is part of this larger algebra.

**Observer's role.** The observer's C-linear measurement maps, when restricted to $M_{16}(\mathbb{R})^{\mathrm{sa}} \subset M_n(\mathbb{C})$, provide the data for a dynamical correspondence. Specifically, the sequential product $T_a\,\&\,T_b = \sqrt{T_a}\,T_b\,\sqrt{T_a} = \tfrac{i}{2}T_b$ (from Route A) shows that the observer's measurement process generates an imaginary Lie-algebraic component. This skew-symmetric part

$$
T_a\,\&\,T_b - T_b\,\&\,T_a = \tfrac{i}{2}T_b - \tfrac{i}{2}T_a \quad (a \ne b,\; \{T_a, T_b\} = 0)
$$

encodes the commutator structure that the Alfsen--Shultz theorem associates with a C*-algebra lifting.

**Conclusion.** The observer's sequential product data, specifically the imaginary factor $i/2$ in $T_a\,\&\,T_b$, constitutes the dynamical correspondence lifting $M_{16}(\mathbb{R})^{\mathrm{sa}}$ to $M_{16}(\mathbb{C})$ via the Alfsen--Shultz theorem. Both Route A and Route B converge on the same target algebra.

**Caveat.** The Alfsen--Shultz theorem applies to *special* JB-algebras (those embeddable in $B(H)$). $M_{16}(\mathbb{R})^{\mathrm{sa}}$ is special; $\mathfrak{h}_3(\mathbb{O})$ is not. The complexification comes from the observer's measurement algebra acting on $V_{1/2}$, not from $\mathfrak{h}_3(\mathbb{O})$ itself.


## 6. Compatibility with Impossibility Theorems

The following impossibility results from Phase 30 remain valid:

**Phase 30, Theorem 1:** $\mathrm{End}_{\mathrm{Spin}(9)}(S_9) = \mathbb{R}$. There is no $\mathrm{Spin}(9)$-equivariant complex structure $J$ on $V_{1/2} = \mathbb{R}^{16}$.

**Status: STILL TRUE.** The observer-induced complexification is NOT equivariant. To see this: the complexification depends on a choice of $T_a$ (the sequential product $\sqrt{T_a}\,T_b\,\sqrt{T_a}$ depends on WHICH generator is the "first measurement"). Different choices of $T_a$ produce different complex structures on $V_{1/2}$. Explicitly: the map $T_b \mapsto i\,T_b$ (extracting the $i$ from the sequential product) is a complex structure on $\mathrm{span}_{\mathbb{R}}\{T_b : b \ne a\}$, but it depends on the choice of $a$. A $\mathrm{Spin}(9)$-equivariant $J$ would be independent of this choice. No such $J$ exists (Phase 30).

**Relationship.** The impossibility theorems establish that complexification CANNOT arise from the algebra $\mathrm{Cl}(9,0)$ alone -- it requires EXTERNAL input. The observer provides this external input through its C*-structure. The self-modeling axioms (Paper 5) mandate that the observer is $M_n(\mathbb{C})^{\mathrm{sa}}$, and this complex structure is what breaks the obstruction. The observer-induced complexification COMPLEMENTS the impossibility theorems; it does not circumvent them.

Specifically:
1. $\mathrm{End}_{\mathrm{Spin}(9)}(S_9) = \mathbb{R}$ remains valid. No equivariant $J$ exists.
2. The observer provides *non-equivariant* external input (its own C*-structure).
3. The sequential product is the specific channel through which this external input acts.
4. The result (complexification of the Peirce operators) requires both the observer AND the Clifford structure -- neither alone is sufficient.

### 7.2 Sign consistency

### 7.3 Effect control

The theorem correctly predicts that effects stay real. For $E_a = \tfrac{1}{2}(I + 2T_a) \in [0, I]$:

$$
\sqrt{E_a} = \sqrt{1} \cdot P_+ + \sqrt{0} \cdot P_- = P_+.
$$

So $E_a\,\&\,E_b = P_+ E_b P_+$. Since $E_b = \tfrac{1}{2}(I + 2T_b) = P_+^{(b)} + 0 \cdot P_-^{(b)}$... more directly: $E_a$ has spectrum $\{1, 0\}$, so $\sqrt{E_a} = P_+$ (the projection onto the eigenvalue-1 space of $E_a$, which equals $P_+$ of $T_a$). Then

$$
E_a\,\&\,E_b = P_+ E_b P_+ = P_+\,\tfrac{1}{2}(I + 2T_b)\,P_+ = \tfrac{1}{2}P_+ + P_+ T_b P_+ = \tfrac{1}{2}P_+.
$$

The last equality uses $P_+ T_b P_+ = 0$ (proved in Step 4 for anticommuting $T_a, T_b$). Since $P_+ = E_a$, we get $E_a\,\&\,E_b = \tfrac{1}{2}E_a$. This is entirely real. Phase 42 confirmed this for all 72 pairs with max error $0.00$.

### 7.4 Diagonal case

For the diagonal sequential product ($a = b$):

$$
\sqrt{T_a}\,T_a\,\sqrt{T_a} = \frac{1}{2}(P_+ + iP_-)\,(\tfrac{1}{2}P_+ - \tfrac{1}{2}P_-)\,(P_+ + iP_-)
$$

$$
= \frac{1}{4}(P_+ + iP_-)\,(P_+ - P_-)\,(P_+ + iP_-)
$$

$$
= \frac{1}{4}(P_+ + iP_-)\,(P_+^2 + iP_+ P_- - P_- P_+ - iP_-^2)
$$

$$
= \frac{1}{4}(P_+ + iP_-)\,(P_+ - iP_-)
$$

$$
= \frac{1}{4}(P_+^2 - iP_+ P_- + iP_- P_+ - i^2 P_-^2)
$$

$$
= \frac{1}{4}(P_+ + P_-) = \frac{1}{4}I_{16}.
$$

Phase 42 verified: $\sqrt{T_a}\,T_a\,\sqrt{T_a} = \tfrac{1}{4}I_{16}$ for all 9 generators, max error $2.23 \times 10^{-16}$.

### 7.5 Impossibility cross-check

The complex structure induced by the sequential product depends on the choice of $T_a$: $J_a(T_b) = i\,T_b$ for $b \ne a$, but $J_a(T_a)$ is not part of this pattern ($T_a\,\&\,T_a = \tfrac{1}{4}I_{16}$, not $i$ times anything). Different choices of $a$ give different linear maps. In particular, $J_a$ does not commute with the $\mathrm{Spin}(9)$ action (which permutes the $T_a$'s among themselves). This confirms the Phase 30 impossibility: no equivariant $J$ exists.
