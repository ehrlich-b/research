\title{Spacetime from Self-Modeling: Einstein's Equations\\from Entanglement Equilibrium on a Self-Modeling Lattice}

\author{Bryan Ehrlich}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
We show that the locality of self-modeling---the principle that a physical
system contains a faithful model of itself, updated through its
boundary---yields Einstein's field equations as the leading-order effective
description at long wavelengths, given lattice topology and
antiferromagnetic coupling as inputs. Self-modeling forces
$M_n(\mathbb{C})^{sa}$ quantum mechanics~\cite{Paper5}. The self-modeling
interaction Hamiltonian $H = \sum_{\langle x,y\rangle} J\, \swapop$,
forced by diagonal $U(n)$ covariance, produces area-law entanglement
entropy. We present two routes to
\begin{equation*}
  \Gab + \Lambda\, \gab = 8\pi \Gnewton\, \Tab
\end{equation*}
with $\Gnewton = 1/(4\eta)$: (A)~Jacobson's entanglement equilibrium
argument~\cite{Jacobson2016}, exact in the conformal regime; and
(B)~Lovelock's uniqueness theorem~\cite{Lovelock1971}, which forces
Einstein's equation in $d \geq 2$ spatial dimensions without requiring
conformal symmetry. The maximal vacuum entanglement hypothesis (MVEH),
traditionally an additional assumption, is identified as the only available
vacuum selection criterion in a pre-geometric framework via the
Connes--Rovelli thermal time hypothesis~\cite{ConnesRovelli1994}.
We present numerical verification on small lattices ($N = 8$--$20$) and
precisely identify remaining inputs: the lattice topology, the
antiferromagnetic coupling sign, and the Wilsonian continuum limit.
\end{abstract}
