\title{Spacetime from Self-Modeling: Einstein's Equations\\from Entanglement Equilibrium on a Self-Modeling Lattice}

\author{Bryan Ehrlich}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}

\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
We show that the locality of self-modeling---the principle that a physical
system contains a faithful model of itself, updated through its
boundary---forces Einstein's field equations as the leading-order effective
description at long wavelengths. Self-modeling forces
$M_n(\mathbb{C})^{sa}$ quantum mechanics~\cite{Paper5}. The self-modeling
interaction Hamiltonian $H = \sum_{\langle x,y\rangle} J\, \swapop$,
forced by diagonal $U(n)$ covariance, produces area-law entanglement
entropy. Applying Jacobson's entanglement equilibrium
argument~\cite{Jacobson2016} in the Wilsonian continuum limit yields
\begin{equation*}
  \Gab + \Lambda\, \gab = 8\pi \Gnewton\, \Tab
\end{equation*}
with $\Gnewton = 1/(4\eta)$, where $\eta$ is the entanglement entropy
density per unit area. The maximal vacuum entanglement hypothesis (MVEH),
traditionally an additional assumption, is reframed as definitional via the
Connes--Rovelli thermal time hypothesis~\cite{ConnesRovelli1994}: in the
absence of pre-existing geometry, the modular flow of the lattice state
\emph{defines} the emergent geometric flow. We present numerical
verification on small lattices ($N = 8$--$20$) and precisely identify
remaining inputs: the lattice topology and the Wilsonian continuum limit.
This paper does not derive Newton's constant~$\Gnewton$, the spacetime
dimension, or the cosmological constant~$\Lambda$.
\end{abstract}
